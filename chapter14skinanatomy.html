Critical Documentation
Always document lesions using precise terminology. "Raised red spot" is inadequate. Use: "3mm erythematous papule with central scale on left forearm." Include: size, color, morphology, texture, and exact location.
‚úì Lesion Classification Correct!
Primary Lesions: Develop initially from disease process
‚Ä¢ Macule: Flat, color change only (freckle, petechiae)
‚Ä¢ Papule: Elevated, solid <1cm (mole, wart)
‚Ä¢ Vesicle: Fluid-filled <1cm (herpes simplex, chickenpox)
‚Ä¢ Pustule: Pus-filled (acne, folliculitis)

Secondary Lesions: Evolve from primary lesions
‚Ä¢ Scale: Flaking skin (psoriasis, dandruff)
‚Ä¢ Crust: Dried serum/blood/pus (impetigo)
‚Ä¢ Erosion: Moist, depressed area (ruptured blister)
‚Ä¢ Ulcer: Full-thickness loss (venous stasis ulcer)

Memory Aid: Primary = Initial presentation, Secondary = After changes (scratching, infection, healing).

Psoriasis:
‚Ä¢ Pathophysiology: T-cell mediated, rapid keratinocyte turnover (3-4 days vs. normal 28)
‚Ä¢ Classic Presentation: Well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery micaceous scale
‚Ä¢ Common Sites: Extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), scalp, sacrum
‚Ä¢ Associated: Nail pitting, arthritis, higher CV risk

‚Ä¢ Pathophysiology: Skin barrier dysfunction, immune dysregulation
‚Ä¢ Classic Presentation: Pruritic, erythematous patches with excoriation, lichenification
‚Ä¢ Common Sites: Flexural areas (antecubital, popliteal), face in children
‚Ä¢ Associated: Personal/family history of atopy (asthma, allergic rhinitis)

‚Ä¢ Pathophysiology: Follicular hyperkeratinization, excess sebum, C. acnes, inflammation
‚Ä¢ Lesions: Comedones (open/closed), inflammatory papules/pustules, nodules/cysts
‚Ä¢ Distribution: Face, chest, back - areas with high sebaceous gland density
‚Ä¢ Treatment Ladder: Topicals ‚Üí oral antibiotics ‚Üí isotretinoin (severe)

‚Ä¢ Subtypes: Erythematotelangiectatic, papulopustular, phymatous, ocular
‚Ä¢ Triggers: Sun, heat, alcohol, spicy foods, stress, certain skincare products
‚Ä¢ Key Feature: Flushing with persistent central facial erythema

ü©∫ Clinical Proficiency Insights

  • Skin Anatomy Mastery: Understanding epidermal-dermal junction is critical for biopsy depth. Shave biopsies should include dermis for adequate diagnosis.
  • Lesion Terminology: "Macule" vs "papule" determines diagnostic pathway. Macules may be monitored; papules often require further evaluation.
  • ABCDE Rule Application: Any single "E" (evolving) criterion warrants referral regardless of other findings. Changing lesions are the most concerning.
  • Inflammatory Pattern Recognition: Psoriasis favors extensor surfaces; eczema favors flexural areas. Distribution pattern is often diagnostic.
  • Documentation Standards: Always measure lesions in centimeters (not inches or descriptive terms like "quarter-sized"). Include color, elevation, texture, and exact anatomic location.

üìã Documentation & Patient Education

  • SOAP Note Structure: Document skin findings in Objective: "8mm asymmetrical pigmented lesion with irregular border and color variegation on left upper back."
  • Patient Education Scripts: "This mole has features that concern me. It's asymmetrical and has multiple colors, which are warning signs. I recommend a biopsy to be safe."
  • Sun Protection Education: Recommend broad-spectrum SPF 30+, reapplied every 2 hours. Physical blockers (zinc/titanium) for sensitive skin or rosacea.
  • Follow-up Timing: High-risk lesions: 2-week follow-up. Moderate risk: 3-6 months. Document patient understanding and agreement with plan.

üö® Red Flags Requiring Immediate Referral

  • Rapidly Growing Lesion: Any lesion doubling in size over weeks requires urgent evaluation.
  • Non-healing Ulcer: Ulcer present >3 weeks without improvement needs biopsy to rule out malignancy.
  • New Lesion After Age 40: Most melanomas are new lesions in older adults.
  • Unexplained Pruritus: Generalized itching without rash can indicate systemic disease (lymphoma, renal/hepatic issues).
  • Blistering Disorders: New onset blistering suggests autoimmune conditions (pemphigus, bullous pemphigoid) requiring dermatology evaluation.
