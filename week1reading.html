Clinical Application

Why Infection Control + Dermatology Matter Together

Chapter 3 (Infection Control) teaches you how to protect everyone in the clinical environment. These protocols are non-negotiable in every healthcare setting and form the foundation of patient safety.

Chapter 14 (Dermatology) shows you how to assess and document the body's largest organ. Skin conditions often signal systemic issues, and dermatologic procedures require meticulous infection control.

Clinical Connections You'll Make:

  • Every dermatology procedure (biopsy, excision, wound care) begins with hand hygiene and ends with proper instrument sterilization.
  • Skin is the first line of defense against pathogensâ€”understanding its structure helps you understand infection prevention.
  • OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard directly applies to dermatology procedures that may involve blood or body fluids.
  • Proper documentation of skin lesions requires the same attention to detail as documenting infection control incidents.
High-Yield Focus Areas:
  • Chain of infection and breakpoints
  • Standard vs. Transmission-Based Precautions
  • ABCDE rule for melanoma detection
  • OSHA requirements for exposure control plans
  • Difference between medical and surgical asepsis
Clinical Insight: In dermatology, never biopsy a suspected melanoma without proper referral. Incomplete removal can spread cancer cells. Always document lesion characteristics with photos when possible and refer to dermatology for suspicious lesions.
