Telephone Triage Sorting Activity

Practice sorting real-world telephone triage calls by urgency. Use what you learned in Chapter 12 – Emergency Readiness and Telephone Triage to decide when to activate EMS, schedule an urgent visit, or give routine advice.

Activity 1

Sort the Calls by Priority

Read each caller scenario and choose the best triage level. Focus on red-flag symptoms, time of onset, and your role as the medical assistant.

A. Call 911 / EMS now B. Have provider/nurse speak with caller immediately C. Schedule same-day urgent office visit D. Give routine advice and schedule non-urgent visit / message
Call 1

A 58-year-old patient reports sudden chest pressure, shortness of breath, and sweating that started 5 minutes ago while walking up stairs.

Call 2

A parent says their 3-year-old has had a low-grade fever and runny nose for two days, but the child is drinking fluids and playing.

Call 3

A patient with a history of diabetes reports new numbness and tingling in both feet over the last week, with no injury.

Call 4

A 72-year-old patient suddenly develops slurred speech and weakness on the right side that began 15 minutes ago.

Call 5

A patient who had a minor ankle sprain yesterday reports increasing swelling and pain, but they can still walk with discomfort.

Call 6

A patient on a new blood pressure medication reports feeling very dizzy and light-headed when standing, and almost fainted this morning.

Apply it

Triage Script Practice

Practice using a structured script

Use this space to draft a brief telephone triage script. Include how you will introduce yourself, confirm the caller’s identity, ask key assessment questions, and close the call.

  • How will you quickly verify the patient’s name, DOB, and call-back number?
  • Which questions will you ask to clarify who, what, when, where, and how severe the problem is?
  • What exact words will you use when you tell someone to call 911 or go to the ED?
