Step 2

Health Record Vocabulary

Tap each card to reveal the definition.

EHR vs Paper Records
EHR: Digital, accessible, structured data. Paper: Physical, single-location, manual filing.
HIPAA
Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act - protects patient privacy and health information
Protected Health Information (PHI)
Any information about health status, treatment, or payment that can identify a patient
Minimum Necessary Rule
Only access/disclose minimum PHI needed to accomplish intended purpose
Charting by Exception
Documenting only abnormal findings; assumes normal findings if not documented
Audit Trail
Electronic record of who accessed/changed a record, when, and what was changed

Types of Health Records:

Problem-Oriented
SOAP format
Source-Oriented
By department
Integrated

Required Documentation Elements:
Patient Identification Date/Time Chief Complaint Vital Signs Assessment Plan Signature Credentials
HIPAA Violation Examples:
• Discussing patients in public areas
• Leaving computer screens unlocked
• Sharing login credentials
• Improper disposal of PHI

Clinical Scenario: New Patient Documentation

Scenario

A 45-year-old female presents for her first visit. She reports headache for 3 days, rates pain 7/10, worse with bright lights. BP: 142/88, HR: 92, Temp: 98.6°F.

Documentation Tasks

  • Identify which information goes in S vs O sections
  • How would you format the SOAP note?
  • What HIPAA considerations apply?
  • How would you correct an error in documentation?

SOAP Classification:

S: "Headache 3 days, 7/10 pain, photophobia"
O: BP 142/88, HR 92, T 98.6
A: Likely migraine
P: Further evaluation
Correct Error Correction:
1. Single line through error
2. Write "error" or "mistaken entry"
3. Initial and date
4. Write correct information
Never use correction fluid or erase!
EHR Advantages:
• Legible documentation
• Clinical decision support
• Automated alerts
• Easy information sharing
• Data analytics capability
